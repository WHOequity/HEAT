Population attributable fraction (PAF)

Definition

PAF shows the potential for improvement in setting average of an indicator, in relative terms, that could be achieved if all population subgroups had the same level of the indicator as a reference group. PAF is a relative measure of inequality that takes into account all population subgroups. It is calculated for all inequality dimensions, provided that all subgroup estimates and subgroup population shares are available. PAF has no unit.

Calculation

PAF is calculated by dividing the difference between the estimate for the reference subgroup y_ref and the setting average Î¼ by the setting average, and multiplying the fraction by 100: PAF=\frac{y_{ref}-Î¼}{Î¼}*100

The reference subgroup y_{ref} depends on the characteristics of the inequality dimension and indicator type. y_{ref} is the most-advantaged subgroup for ordered dimensions. For non-ordered dimensions, it is the subgroup with the highest estimate for favourable indicators and is the subgroup with the lowest estimate for adverse indicators. If a specific reference subgroup has been specified (using the reference_subgroup field in the HEAT template) then y_{ref} refers to this subgroup.

The variance of PAF is calculated as: var(PAF)= \frac{cN[ad(N-c)+bc^2]}{(a+c)^3 (c+d)^3} where a, b, c, d and N are numbers of people based on a 2x2 contingency table (Table 3).

Interpretation

PAF takes positive values for favourable indicators and negative values for adverse indicators. The larger the absolute value of PAF, the larger the level of inequality. PAF is zero if no further improvement can be achieved, i.e. if all subgroups have reached the same level of the indicator as the reference subgroup.

Example

The graph shows that, overall, coverage increased in all quintiles and inequality between quintiles reduced over time. PAF measures the potential improvement in overall coverage of skilled birth attendance that could be achieved if all quintiles had the same level of coverage as quintile 5, i.e. if there was no economic-related inequality. Figure b shows that setting average could have been 97.3% higher in 2005 and 28.3% higher in 2010 if there had been no economic-related inequality. PAF decreased between 2005 and 2010 indicating a decrease in relative economic-related inequality.
